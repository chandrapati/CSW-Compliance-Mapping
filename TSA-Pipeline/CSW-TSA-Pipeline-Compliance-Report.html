How to read this report

This report describes how Cisco Secure Workload (CSW) supports a TSA-designated pipeline owner/operator’s compliance posture against the TSA Security Directive Pipeline-2021-02 series cybersecurity requirements on the IT side of the IT/OT boundary.

Scope statement up front. CSW is a workload-protection and segmentation platform for IT estate — servers, virtual machines, containers, and cloud workloads. CSW does not enforce policy on RTUs, flow computers, PLCs, valve actuators, or any other Level 0–2 OT device on the pipeline itself. CSW’s TSA SD contribution is at the IT-side of the IT/OT boundary and within the IT systems that support pipeline operations — engineering workstations, SCADA jump servers, historian databases, MES/EAM systems, vendor remote- access hosts, identity/PKI services, and the corporate IT systems that touch Critical Cyber Systems on the IT side.

Companion document. The accompanying TSA Pipeline Technical Runbook walks through deployment, scoping, IT/OT segmentation design, access control, continuous monitoring, vulnerability management on the IT side, the Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan evidence pack, and the Cybersecurity Assessment Plan inputs. Read this report for the audit-narrative view; read the runbook for the deployment view.

Note on revision letters. TSA has revised SD Pipeline-2021-02 multiple times (02, 02A, 02B, 02C, 02D, …). The substantive controls in the III.A–III.D sections have been stable in shape but have been refined in detail. This report is written generically to the current effective revision; reconcile against the active version on the TSA web site before relying on this document in an inspection response.

1. Executive Summary

The TSA Pipeline Security Directive 2021-02 series imposes mandatory cybersecurity requirements on TSA-designated Critical Pipeline owner/operators in the United States. The directive’s substantive cybersecurity content sits in four sections (III.A through III.D) that line up cleanly with the IT-side capabilities of a workload- protection platform: segmentation, access control, continuous monitoring, and reduction of unpatched-system risk.

What CSW changes for a TSA SD programme:

  • Section III.A — IT/OT segmentation evidence. The IT/OT firewall remains your boundary, but CSW enforces deny-by-default up to that boundary on the IT side. Undocumented IT pivots toward the IT/OT boundary are structurally removed.
  • Section III.A — Documented data flows. CSW provides the observed-vs-documented IT→OT flow inventory that lets architecture reviews focus on gaps and changes rather than rebuilding the as-is picture.
  • Section III.B — Access control evidence. Workload-level least- privilege with quarterly access review and sunset logs for unused rules.
  • Section III.C — Continuous monitoring. Behavioural detections, conversation-graph anomalies, and per-flow telemetry to your SIEM, paired with your OT-aware sensor stack for end-to-end coverage.
  • Section III.D — Unpatched-system risk reduction. Continuous CVE inventory per workload with CVSS, exploit availability, and EPSS context; compensating controls registered for delayed patches.
  • CIRP and CAP evidence. Six-artefact incident reconstruction bundles for your Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan; per-section evidence pack supporting the annual Cybersecurity Assessment Plan self-assessment.

What CSW does not change. CSW does not perform Critical Cyber System designation, does not enforce on OT devices, does not perform deep packet inspection of OT protocols (DNP3, IEC 61850, Modbus, OPC-UA), and does not author the Cybersecurity Architecture Design Review or the CIRP. It is the IT-side evidence engine that those governance artefacts depend on.

Posture takeaway. CSW provides direct IT-side coverage for all four substantive cybersecurity sections (III.A through III.D), the Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan evidence requirement, and the Cybersecurity Assessment Plan self-assessment. It is supporting on Section II inventory and architecture review inputs, and explicitly out of scope for OT-device-layer controls and physical/HR scope. This is consistent with CSW’s positioning as a workload-protection platform: the IT side of the SD is where it earns its place.

CSW’s role in a complete TSA SD programme is the IT-side segmentation, monitoring, and continuous evidence engine. It explicitly defers the OT device-layer scope to the appropriate specialised stack and the governance / HR / physical scope to the appropriate owners.

5. Suggested Next Steps

For TSA-designated pipeline owner/operators considering CSW within their SD programme:

  1. Confirm CCS inputs. Validate the Critical Cyber System list and the OT-facing IT tier definition before starting CSW scope design. CSW labels mirror this taxonomy; bad inputs produce bad evidence.
  1. Pilot on the OT-facing IT tier first. SCADA jump servers, historian/MES, engineering workstations, and vendor-access hosts give the highest-value wins (Section III.A IT/OT segmentation, Section III.B access control, Section III.C continuous monitoring) with the lowest deployment friction.
  2. Pair with the existing IT/OT firewall. CSW does not replace the IT/OT firewall. Position CSW as the IT-side hardening up to the firewall, with the firewall remaining the boundary control of record under Section III.A.
  3. Wire the observed-vs-documented IT→OT flow report into the architecture review cycle. This is the artefact most likely to surface stale documentation and undocumented operational flows.
  4. Tie the compensating-control register to the patch programme day one. The Section III.D requirement is most often missed when patches are delayed for operational reasons; the CSW compensating-control register is the audit-ready evidence that risk has been reduced, not just deferred.
  5. Use the CAP evidence pack to drive the annual self-assessment. The pack is structured per SD section and produces a prior- period delta automatically, which simplifies the annual review.
  6. Plan SME review. This document is a v1 mapping. Before being relied upon in a formal compliance engagement, route to a TSA Pipeline cybersecurity specialist for regulatory accuracy and to a current Cisco Secure Workload SE for product-capability accuracy.

6. Disclaimer

This report and its companion runbook describe how Cisco Secure Workload capabilities can support a TSA Pipeline Security Directive cybersecurity programme on the IT side of the IT/OT boundary. They are not legal or regulatory advice and do not constitute a finding of compliance.

Specifically:

  • TSA Security Directives are issued and revised by the Transportation Security Administration; the active revision letter (02, 02A, 02B, 02C, 02D, …) and the substantive requirements evolve. Always validate against the current effective revision available from TSA, against your inspector’s current guidance, and against any subsequent rulemaking that supersedes the SD.
  • Designation as a Critical Pipeline owner/operator is a TSA determination. Identification of Critical Cyber Systems, designation of the Cybersecurity Coordinator, the design of the Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan and the Cybersecurity Assessment Plan, Cybersecurity Architecture Design Review decisions, and incident reporting decisions remain the responsibility of the owner/ operator’s Senior Cybersecurity Officer and qualified compliance counsel.
  • CSW does not enforce policy on RTUs, flow computers, PLCs, IEDs, HMIs, or any other OT device, and is not certified as an OT- protocol deep-packet-inspection or architecture review tool.

This document should receive subject-matter-expert review for both TSA SD regulatory accuracy and current Cisco product capability before being used in a customer engagement.
