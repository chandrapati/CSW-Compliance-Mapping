How to read this report

This report describes how Cisco Secure Workload (CSW) supports a Registered Entity’s compliance posture against the NERC Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) Reliability Standards on the IT side of the IT/OT boundary.

Scope statement up front. CSW is a workload-protection and segmentation platform for IT estate — servers, virtual machines, containers, and cloud workloads. CSW does not enforce policy on PLCs, RTUs, relays, IEDs, HMIs, or any other Level 0–2 OT device. CSW’s NERC CIP contribution is at the IT-side of the ESP boundary and within the Electronic Access Control or Monitoring Systems (EACMS) and Physical Access Control Systems (PACS) supporting infrastructure — jump hosts, identity/PKI servers, vendor-access hosts, patch repositories, engineering workstations, historian/MES databases, and the corporate IT systems that touch BES Cyber System Information (BCSI). For controls inside the ESP at the OT device layer, your ICS/SCADA security stack and your ESP-boundary firewall arrangement remain the controlling artefacts.

Companion document. The accompanying NERC CIP Technical Runbook walks through deployment, scoping, ESP-boundary design, IRA enforcement, CIP-007/010 evidence collection, and CIP-013 supply- chain visibility on the IT side. Read this report for the audit- narrative view; read the runbook for the deployment view.

1. Executive Summary

NERC CIP cybersecurity requirements apply to Registered Entities operating the Bulk Electric System in North America. The standards have moved decisively since 2020 toward continuous evidence, automated configuration baselining, and supply-chain visibility — all areas where workload-level segmentation and telemetry produce materially better RSAW responses than network-firewall logs alone.

What CSW changes for a NERC CIP programme:

  • CIP-005 R1 IT-side hardening. The ESP boundary firewall remains your EAP, but CSW enforces deny-by-default up to that boundary from the corporate IT side. The undocumented-pivot risk that has driven several past enforcement actions is structurally removed.
  • CIP-005 R2 Interactive Remote Access evidence. Per-session flow attribution from the Intermediate System out to the BES Cyber Asset, retained for the audit window, with no forensic gaps.
  • CIP-007 R1 Ports and Services evidence. Per-workload listening- port inventory with last-observed-flow timestamp, cross-referenced to the documented baseline, dated quarterly.
  • CIP-010 R1.5 Configuration Change Management. Daily diff of installed software and listening ports, with disposition column tying back to your change-management ticketing system. Orphan changes (CSW-detected but ticket-absent) become unauthorised- change candidates.
  • CIP-010 R3 Vulnerability Assessments. Continuous CVE inventory per workload with CVSS, exploit-availability, and EPSS context; cadence-based exports satisfy R3.1 active VA evidence for High- impact assets.
  • CIP-013 R1 Supply Chain Risk Management. Vendor-egress observation against your vendor register, with allowlist enforcement on EACMS and immediate alerting on new external destinations.

What CSW does not change. CSW does not perform CIP-002 categorisation, does not act as an EAP, does not enforce on OT devices, and does not replace the CIP Senior Manager’s accountability under CIP-003. It is the IT-side evidence engine that those governance artefacts depend on.

  • Direct — CSW is the primary technical control or evidence source for the requirement on the IT side.
  • Supporting — CSW supplements another control with evidence or enforcement that strengthens the audit response.
  • Out of scope — CSW does not address the requirement; relies on other parts of the cyber programme (HR, physical, governance, OT controls).

Posture takeaway. CSW provides direct coverage on six of the fourteen standards (CIP-005, CIP-007, CIP-008, CIP-010, CIP-013) on the IT side, supporting evidence on three more, and is structurally out of scope on the remaining five (HR, physical, OT-device-layer controls). This is consistent with CSW’s positioning as a workload- protection platform: it does the IT-side jobs well and explicitly defers OT-device and physical/HR scope to the appropriate owners.

CSW’s role in a complete NERC CIP programme is the IT-side segmentation, EACMS hardening, and continuous evidence engine. It explicitly defers the OT device-layer scope to the appropriate specialised stack and the governance / HR / physical scope to the appropriate owners.

5. Suggested Next Steps

For Registered Entities considering CSW within their NERC CIP programme:

  1. Confirm CIP-002 inputs. Validate the BCS impact ratings and the EACMS / PACS-supporting / BCSI-host tier definition before starting CSW scope design. CSW labels mirror this taxonomy; bad inputs produce bad evidence.
  1. Pilot on the EACMS tier first. Jump hosts, vendor-access hosts, AD, PKI, and patch repositories give the highest-value wins (CIP-005 R1 IT-side, CIP-005 R2 IRA evidence, CIP-007 R1 ports inventory) with the lowest deployment friction.
  2. Pair with the existing EAP firewall pair. CSW does not replace the EAP. Position CSW as the IT-side hardening up to the EAP, with the EAP itself remaining the boundary control of record under CIP-005 R1.
  3. Wire the CIP-010 R1.5 unauthorised-change feed into your ticketing system day one. The orphan-change report is one of the most asked-for artefacts during RSAW evidence requests, and CSW produces it automatically.
  4. Tie the CIP-013 vendor-egress allowlist to your supplier onboarding workflow. Adding CSW allowlist updates as a step in vendor onboarding closes the most common audit gap (vendor endpoint observed but not in the register).
  5. Plan SME review. This document is a v1 mapping. Before being relied upon in a formal compliance engagement, route to a NERC CIP compliance specialist for regulatory accuracy and to a current Cisco Secure Workload SE for product-capability accuracy.

6. Disclaimer

This report and its companion runbook describe how Cisco Secure Workload capabilities can support a NERC CIP cybersecurity programme on the IT side of the IT/OT boundary. They are not legal or regulatory advice and do not constitute a finding of compliance.

Specifically:

  • Final identification and categorisation of BES Cyber Systems (CIP-002), the design of ESP and EAP arrangements (CIP-005), determination of impact ratings (Attachment 1), CIP-008 reportable Cyber Security Incident decisions (R4), and the formal RSAW audit response remain the responsibility of the Registered Entity’s CIP Senior Manager and qualified compliance counsel, working with the applicable Regional Entity (WECC, NPCC, RF, SERC, TRE, MRO).
  • CSW does not enforce policy on PLCs, RTUs, IEDs, HMIs, or any other Level 0–2 OT device, and is not certified as an Electronic Access Point (EAP).
  • Cisco product capabilities, NERC standards effective revisions, and Regional Entity audit guidance evolve. Always validate against the current effective revision of each CIP standard published on the NERC web site, against your Regional Entity’s current audit guidance, and against any FERC orders amending applicability or compliance dates.

This document should receive subject-matter-expert review for both NERC CIP regulatory accuracy and current Cisco product capability before being used in a customer engagement.
